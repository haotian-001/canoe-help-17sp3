MATLAB Suspension Control

 

Click on the blue link to load the sample configuration in CANoe.

  • Suspension Control – Suspension_Control_CAN_2ms.cfg
    Shows the draft of an active chassis control system including a vehicle model.

The purpose of the vehicle model is to enable simulation of adequately realistic driving dynamics and, together with active dampers, serve as a platform for the chassis control. This control will be used to achieve driving performance that is as comfortable as possible. For this reason, the overall model is divided into two main blocks:

1) Environment model (environment simulation for controller) including:

  • Multi-body model of the vehicle (mechanical vehicle model)
  • Model of the drive train (simplified engine model)
  • Model of the brakes
  • Four wheel models
  • Roadway model

2) Chassis control including:

  • Vehicle observer
  • Higher-level chassis controller
  • Four lower-level force controllers for the active dampers

The environment model is subdivided into the vehicle model and the roadway model. The vehicle model is designed as a multi-body model with 15 degrees of freedom. The multi-body model is defined symbolically using a computer algebra system, and the motion equations are derived from this (approximately 4,400 additions and 20,800 multiplications). The vehicle model provides additional inputs for stimulation: gas and brake pedal positions, the gear engaged, steering angle, hand brake position, and controller required value specification (comfort or sport). The communication of these values is realized in CANoe using system variables. The driver specifications can be entered interactively on a panel with corresponding controls. Using recorded macros, it is also possible to play back an automated driving profile. The roadway model is modeled by a lookup table that contains the roadway surface height and the surface normals for every position. Likewise, the surface condition is described by its coefficient of friction at every position.

The chassis control consists of an LQ controller with observer. The controller calculates the setpoint forces for the active force elements in the wheel suspensions. The control has two main objectives:

  • Reduction of the vehicle body accelerations.
  • Result for the driver: increased comfort.
  • Keeping the wheel contact area constant (minimization of the variation of the wheel contact forces).
  • Result for the driver: increased safety and improved driving dynamics.

The observer reconstructs the vehicle conditions that cannot be measured and uses a simplified linear vehicle model (seven degrees of freedom) for this. The chassis controller requires the roll and pitch angle accelerations of the vehicle body from two gyro sensors as well as the lifting acceleration of the vehicle body from an acceleration sensor as measured quantities. The quantities are made available by the vehicle simulation and are likewise communicated in CANoe to the chassis controller using system variables. The force controller controls the force of the active force elements to their setpoint as specified by the chassis control. This is designed, for example, as a simple PI controller for current control.

Six simulation nodes are defined in CANoe (Figure: Model of an active chassis control). These are the environment simulation node (vehicle model), the chassis controller node, and four force controller nodes.

The six nodes exchange the four setpoint forces for the force controller and the four deflections of the wheel suspensions using system variables.
